# Discovery Call Transcript
## Henderson & Associates — Client Intake Process Review
**Date:** April 10, 2026
**Present:** Laura Henderson (Managing Partner), Russ (Consultant)

---

**Russ:** Laura, walk me through what happens when a potential new client calls your office.

**Laura:** Sure. So the phone rings and Tamika picks up. She's our receptionist. She takes down the basics — name, phone number, what kind of accident, when it happened. She's got a little form she fills out, just a Word doc she prints every morning. Then she checks the statute of limitations. We've got a chart taped to the wall next to her desk — two years for most personal injury, but workers' comp and med mal are different. She looks it up and if it seems like it's within the window, she transfers the call to Maria.

**Russ:** Maria Ruiz, your senior paralegal?

**Laura:** Right. Maria's been with me fifteen years. She's family. If Maria's on another call, Tamika takes a message and Maria's supposed to call back within 24 hours. Honestly? Sometimes it's two days. Sometimes three. She's juggling forty active cases by herself. And that's the problem — I think we're losing clients because by the time Maria calls back, they've already hired another firm. Whoever calls back first wins.

**Russ:** So when Maria does connect with them, what happens?

**Laura:** She does a screening call. Asks about the accident — where it happened, who was at fault, witnesses, what injuries, are they still treating. She's got maybe eight or ten questions. She knows within two minutes if it's a real case. But here's the thing — and don't tell Maria I said this — sometimes she spends forty-five minutes on the phone with someone who clearly isn't a case. Someone calls in crying because they fell at Walmart and their knee hurts, and Maria's being a therapist for an hour. She's too empathetic. It's her best quality and her worst quality.

**Russ:** After the screening, what's next?

**Laura:** She checks for conflicts of interest. She's got a spreadsheet of all our current and former clients — fifteen years of names — and she manually searches through it. Sorts by name, scrolls around. Takes her about ten minutes. And honestly, I worry. What if she misses one? That's a malpractice risk. The bar association takes conflicts very seriously. You can get disbarred for that.

**Russ:** Then what?

**Laura:** If it's clear, she enters everything into Clio — that's our case management system. Contact info, accident details, injuries, all of it. Takes her another fifteen, twenty minutes per case. Pure data entry. Then she sends the retainer through DocuSign. That part works great actually. Our one technology win. [laughs]

**Russ:** What about after someone becomes a client? Any follow-up?

**Laura:** Maria's supposed to make check-in calls at seven days, fourteen days, and thirty days. See how the client's doing, make sure they're keeping doctor appointments, let them know where things stand. Those calls matter — clients who feel ignored start calling other lawyers. Maria told me once, "These people are hurting and scared and the least we can do is pick up the phone." I can't argue with that. But the seven-day call usually happens, fourteen-day is fifty-fifty, and thirty-day... it falls off. There's just too much on her plate.

**Russ:** When you mentioned AI to Maria, how did that go?

**Laura:** [laughs] She said, and I quote, "I don't trust a robot to talk to someone who just got hit by a truck." And I get it. But we can't keep going like this. Whatever we do, just — make it something I can explain to Maria without her walking out.

**Russ:** Budget and timeline?

**Laura:** Derek, our office manager, says two thousand a month. And he's very clear — nothing can violate attorney-client privilege. Everything between us and our clients is confidential. I'd like to see something working in thirty days.
